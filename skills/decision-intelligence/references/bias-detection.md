# Bias Detection

## Cognitive Bias Taxonomy

This reference lists the most common cognitive biases that affect organizational
decision-making, detection patterns, and mitigation strategies.

---

## Anchoring Bias

**Definition**: Over-reliance on the first piece of information encountered.

**Detection patterns**:
- First option presented is the clear winner without thorough alternatives analysis
- Price or estimate anchors early in the evaluation ("the vendor said $100K, so let's evaluate around that")
- One stakeholder's initial position dominates discussion without challenge

**Mitigation**:
- Present options without ordering by preference
- Ask each evaluator to score independently before group discussion
- Explicitly solicit "steelman" arguments for each alternative

---

## Confirmation Bias

**Definition**: Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs; discounting contrary evidence.

**Detection patterns**:
- Evidence search stopped once the preferred conclusion is supported
- Risks of the preferred option are listed as "manageable" with no specifics
- Alternative options' weaknesses receive more scrutiny than the preferred option's

**Mitigation**:
- Assign a "devil's advocate" role to challenge the preferred option
- Require equal evidence documentation for all options
- Ask: "What would have to be true for each alternative to be the right choice?"

---

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Definition**: Continuing to invest in a failing course of action because of prior investment.

**Detection patterns**:
- "We've already invested $X, we can't stop now"
- Decision brief mentions prior investment as a reason to continue
- Reversibility of the decision is rated lower because of existing commitments

**Mitigation**:
- Explicitly ask: "If we were starting fresh today, would we make this choice?"
- Separate the question of prior investment from the forward-looking decision
- Require cost-to-completion analysis vs cost-to-abandon

---

## Availability Bias

**Definition**: Overweighting recent or memorable events in probability estimates.

**Detection patterns**:
- Risk estimates based on a single recent incident rather than historical rates
- A recent high-profile failure causes excessive risk aversion
- A recent success causes overconfidence in similar approaches

**Mitigation**:
- Use base rates (historical data) to anchor probability estimates
- Explicitly ask: "What's the long-run historical rate of this outcome?"
- Check if the triggering event is statistically significant or an outlier

---

## Groupthink

**Definition**: Desire for harmony or conformity overrides realistic appraisal of alternatives.

**Detection patterns**:
- No one voices objections in a group meeting
- Contrary views were expressed privately but not in the decision meeting
- Decision appears unanimous without genuine debate

**Mitigation**:
- Collect individual assessments before group discussion
- Designate a formal devil's advocate for each significant decision
- Create psychological safety to challenge the prevailing view
- Anonymous scoring before group discussion

---

## Overconfidence Bias

**Definition**: Excessive confidence in one's own answers and forecasts.

**Detection patterns**:
- Confidence intervals are too narrow (engineer says "2 weeks" with no uncertainty)
- "Known unknowns" are not listed despite complex scope
- Risk list is very short for a complex decision

**Mitigation**:
- Require confidence intervals, not point estimates
- Use pre-mortem: "It's one year from now and this decision failed — why?"
- Reference class forecasting: how long did similar projects actually take?

---

## Status Quo Bias

**Definition**: Preference for the current state; change is perceived as a loss.

**Detection patterns**:
- "Do nothing" option is not seriously evaluated
- Risks of change are listed extensively; risks of not changing are absent
- Decision brief frames the choice as "change vs risk" rather than "two options with different profiles"

**Mitigation**:
- Explicitly evaluate the cost and risk of not changing
- Frame as: "Option A: change. Option B: stay the same (with these risks and costs)"
- Ask: "What would this look like in 12 months if we don't act?"

---

## Bias Detection Checklist

Before finalizing any significant decision, check:

- [ ] Were options presented in a neutral order without pre-ranking?
- [ ] Did each evaluator score independently before group discussion?
- [ ] Is the preferred option's risks analyzed as rigorously as the alternatives'?
- [ ] Are probability estimates based on historical data, not just recent events?
- [ ] Was the cost of not deciding or maintaining status quo explicitly evaluated?
- [ ] Is there a devil's advocate voice in this decision?
- [ ] Are confidence intervals provided (not just point estimates)?
- [ ] If any of the biases above were flagged: is the decision owner aware?
